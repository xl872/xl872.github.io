# Hi, I'm Xiaochen Liu

I am a **Postdoctoral Fellow** at the **Athinoula A. Martinos Center for Biomedical Imaging, Massachusetts General Hospital & Harvard Medical School**. My research focuses on **quantitative neurovascular imaging** and the **physiological interpretation of fMRI in awake mice**, with an emphasis on improving the specificity, reproducibility, and interpretability of brain-wide functional and vascular readouts.

My long-term goal is to develop principled neuroimaging and analysis frameworks that link brain-wide fMRI measurements to underlying **vascular** and **neuromodulatory** mechanisms, and to apply these tools to understand **cognitive dysfunction** and **neurodegenerative disease**.

## Current Research

### Ultra-high-field awake mouse fMRI
I work on ultra-high-field awake mouse fMRI with synchronized physiological measurements to better separate neural, vascular, and systemic contributions to fMRI signals. This includes the development and application of robust motion and artifact handling strategies for high-resolution imaging in awake animals.

### Neuromodulatory dysfunction in Alzheimer’s disease
Using **pupillary light response-driven brain-wide fMRI**, I study neuromodulatory circuit function and its disruption in Alzheimer’s disease models. I am also interested in combining imaging biomarkers with **CNN-based classification** and **explainable AI** approaches to identify disease-relevant features.

### Quantitative cerebrovascular mapping
My work also includes **CBV MRI-derived vessel metrics** and super-resolution-based microvascular analysis to characterize region-specific vascular abnormalities in transgenic Alzheimer’s disease mice.

### Vasodynamics and neurovascular coupling
I study low-frequency vascular rhythms and their spatiotemporal organization as a window into vascular tone and neurovascular coupling in awake brain states, including propagation patterns and structured vasodynamic activity.

### State-transition fMRI
I contribute to within-subject **state-transition fMRI** paradigms bridging anesthetized and awake imaging, aiming to isolate arousal-related effects and improve mechanistic interpretation across brain states.
